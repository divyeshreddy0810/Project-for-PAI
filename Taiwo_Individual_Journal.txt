WHAT THIS JOURNAL IS
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

This is my personal account of building the MRAT-RL system with my
group. It records what I specifically worked on, the problems I ran

not a summary of the group report. Where the report explains what we

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
GETTING STARTED: SCOPE AND DATA
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

When we sat down as a group to decide what to build, the first
disagreement was about scope. Some ideas started at five equities.
I pushed for something broader. My reasoning was simple: if the whole
point of the project is to test whether one AI system can handle
fundamentally different asset types, then five equities of the same
type is not actually a test of that. You need breadth.

My contribution to the scope decision was proposing the African forex
pairs — USD/NGN, USD/GHS, USD/ZAR, USD/KES. These are not in the
standard G10 set that most academic trading papers use. They are
volatile, less liquid, and driven by completely different macro
conditions than EUR/USD or GBP/USD. Including them was a deliberate
choice to stress-test the system on currencies that would not be
treated kindly by a model built around major Western pairs.

Once the scope was agreed, data collection fell to me. I built the
pipeline using the yfinance library to pull daily OHLCV data for all
48 assets. One thing I made sure to do that we had not planned for
initially was set up a proper SQLite database rather than just saving
everything as CSV files. The reason was practical: 48 assets times 22
years of daily data is a lot of files. Querying a database by ticker
and date range is much faster than loading a folder of CSVs every time
the advisory pipeline runs. I designed three tables — ohlcv for raw
prices, features for the engineered feature vectors, and lab_results
for the Deep Learning Laboratory output — and wrote a connection helper
that handles opens, commits, and closes cleanly to prevent lock
contention across multi-session training runs.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
FEATURE ENGINEERING: THE SENTIMENT PROXY
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Most of the feature engineering was collaborative. My specific piece
was the sentiment proxy — dimension 14 in the RL agent state vector.
We did not have access to a third-party sentiment feed, so I built one
from what we already had. The proxy combines two signals: Bollinger
Band position (where the closing price sits relative to the upper and
lower bands) and volume divergence (current day volume versus the
20-day average). The idea is that when price is near the upper band and
volume is spiking above average, the market is showing the kind of
momentum that often precedes a continuation. When price is near the
lower band and volume is unusually high, it can signal panic or
capitulation. Neither signal is reliable alone. Together, as a
normalised composite, they add something the pure price-based features
do not capture.

I also integrated the VIX and FRED interest rate series as macro
features (dimensions 11 and 12 of the state vector). The VIX matters
because it is a market-wide fear indicator — an agent that does not
know whether the overall market is panicking or calm is working with
incomplete context. The interest rate series matters especially for
the 2022 backtest period, which was almost entirely driven by the
Federal Reserve's rate hike cycle.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
THE BUG THAT COST US A FULL RETRAINING RUN
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

The first time I ran the full advisory pipeline after a complete
training run, the output made no sense. The 5-day return forecasts were
showing -45% to -49% across multiple assets. Those are not just
large numbers. They are physically impossible for daily-rebalanced
instruments. A stock index would have to lose nearly half its value in
a working week. It has never happened outside the 1929 crash and even
then not at that speed.

My first thought was a data normalisation error — the scaler fitted on
the wrong split, or values leaking through in the wrong units. I spent
time checking that first. It was not the problem.

The actual cause was simpler and more embarrassing: the RL agents had
been initialised with state_dim=17, but the trading environment was
providing 19-dimensional state vectors. Two features — current
portfolio position and unrealised profit/loss — had been added to the
environment after the agent code was originally written. Nobody updated
the agent constructors to match.

PyTorch did not raise an error. It accepted the mismatched input,
reshaped internally, and ran calculations on malformed data. The output
looked like numbers. They were noise.

I traced it by adding print statements to output the shape of the state
vector at inference time. The moment the sizes printed on screen —
torch.Size([19]) from the environment, 17 from the agents — the cause
was obvious. The fix was two lines of code, one per constructor that
needed changing (three agents in total).

The cost was a full retraining run across all 48 assets. That took
roughly 40 hours on a single consumer GPU. Not a comfortable lesson,
but a permanent one: AI systems do not always announce when something
is wrong. If the output numbers look like numbers, they will pass
unexamined. The only check that caught this was asking whether the
numbers made physical sense in the real world.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
MANAGING THE TRAINING RUNS
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

I launched and monitored the final cold-start retrain. Before starting,
I had to resolve a path issue that had silently affected an earlier run.

The training script uses relative paths to save model checkpoints. If
you run it from the project root, the path data/models/ resolves
correctly and the advisory pipeline can find the files. If you run it
from inside the scripts/ subdirectory — which is a natural thing to do
when working in that folder — the path resolves to
scripts/data/models/, a completely different location that the
advisory pipeline does not check.

I found this by comparing file creation timestamps. The root
data/models/ directory had models dated from early April. The
scripts/data/models/ directory had models with today's date. The
current training run had been writing to the wrong place the whole
time. The fix was to always run from the project root and verify the
output path before starting any training run.

For the final retrain, I monitored progress asset by asset. The large
equity indices finished first — S&P 500, NASDAQ, Dow Jones. These have
5611 rows of data going back to 2004, which gives the agents real
history to learn from across multiple market cycles. The SAC agent on
the S&P 500 settled at a Sharpe ratio of 0.595. Modest, but that is
exactly what you would expect from the most efficiently priced asset in
the world. A very high Sharpe on an index like that would mean the
model had found an exploitable pattern that thousands of professional
quants have missed for decades. That is almost never the right
explanation.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
THE COREWEAVE PROBLEM
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

CoreWeave (CRWV) produced a training Sharpe ratio of 6.009.

My immediate reaction was not to report it as a result. A Sharpe of 6
is the kind of number that appears in academic fraud cases, not in
well-built systems trained on real market data. TSLA produced 1.263.
MU produced 1.228. Those are strong results on genuinely volatile
assets. A number four times that, on a company that listed in March
2025, demanded an explanation.

The explanation was simple: CoreWeave had 268 rows of daily price data
at training time. The 70/15/15 chronological split left fewer than 40
rows in the test set. The LightGBM model for CRWV produced a best
iteration count of 1 for two of its four forecast horizons — LightGBM's
way of saying it could not find any useful split points in the data.

A model trained on 268 rows can memorise the sequence it saw. That is
not learning. It is transcription. A Sharpe ratio computed on 40
out-of-sample rows is not a measure of generalisation. It is a measure
of how well the model remembered the specific path that asset took
during a short window.

CRWV was kept in the system for completeness but excluded from all
headline performance figures. Its model confidence is automatically
suppressed by an earnings proximity flag. That was the right call, and
it was better to catch it during analysis than to report it as a
genuine result.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
DEEP LEARNING LABORATORY
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

I ran the 1200-experiment grid search comparing MLP, CNN, LSTM, and
PatchTST across all 48 assets. Each run wrote its result to a JSON log
immediately on completion — a crash-safe design that allowed the
laboratory to resume from the last completed experiment after any
interruption rather than starting over.

The result that surprised me most was how completely MLP failed. It did
not underperform on some assets and win on others. It never promoted.
Not once across all 48 assets and all hyperparameter configurations.
A flat network with no temporal structure, even with LayerNorm and
dropout, simply cannot compete on time series data with architectures
that are built to use the ordering of the sequence.

The split between CNN and LSTM was more interesting. CNN dominated
cryptocurrencies. LSTM did well on equities, particularly the volatile
semiconductor names. PatchTST held its ground on major forex pairs
where long-range session dependencies across multiple trading days
matter more than local patterns. This was not a random distribution of
winners. Each outcome reflected something real about the structure of
the underlying data.


-----------------------------------------------------------------------
WHAT I ACTUALLY LEARNED
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Three things I will carry forward from this project that no lecture
could have given me directly:

The first is that a system that fails silently is more dangerous than
one that crashes. The state dimension bug produced output on every run.
It printed Sharpe ratios, it saved model files, it generated trading
signals. All of it was meaningless. The only test that caught it was
human judgment about whether the numbers made physical sense.

The second is that scope decisions have invisible costs that only
become visible at runtime. Adding each asset class felt like a small
decision on a whiteboard. The actual cost was a 40-hour training run,
five separate HMMs, different RL agents per volatility profile, and a
database that needed to stay consistent across all of them. Scope
decides complexity. Complexity decides how much time the bugs will cost.

The third is that restraint is not timidity. The system held 59.6 per
cent of positions as HOLD on its evaluation date. That is not the
system being cautious by design. It is the system having learned,
across 7800 test windows and 22 years of market history, that the cost
of acting without conviction exceeds the cost of waiting. The agents
were not told to be conservative. They figured it out. That is the
part that felt genuinely like artificial intelligence rather than
engineering.
